The FTC Guidelines: Understanding "Clear and Conspicuous"

The Federal Trade Commission's .com Disclosures guide sets the global standard for affiliate disclosures. Their requirements boil down to two key concepts:

1. Proximity Matters

Disclosures must be "as close as possible" to the affiliate links they refer to. The FTC specifically warns against:

  • Burying disclosures in terms of service
  • Placing disclosures only in footers or sidebars
  • Using hyperlinks that say "disclosure" without context
  • Separating disclosures from claims with unrelated content

2. Clarity is Non-Negotiable

Language must be understandable to the "average consumer." This means:

  • Avoiding technical or legal jargon
  • Using simple, direct language
  • Being unambiguous about the commercial relationship
  • Making disclosures visible on all devices (mobile included)

FTC-Approved Disclosure Examples

Good: "Disclosure: This post contains affiliate links. I earn a commission if you make a purchase through my links, at no extra cost to you."

Better: "I recommend products I genuinely love. When you purchase through my affiliate links (like the one below), I earn a small commission at no extra cost to you."

Amazon Specific: "As an Amazon Associate I earn from qualifying purchases." (Required by Amazon Associates)

Social Media Disclosures: Platform-Specific Guidelines

Each social platform has unique requirements for affiliate disclosures:

Instagram & Facebook

  • Use #ad or #affiliate in the first three lines of captions
  • Don't bury hashtags among dozens of others
  • Use Instagram's "Paid Partnership" tag when available
  • In Stories, overlay text saying "Affiliate link" on the link sticker

YouTube

  • Verbal disclosure within the first 30 seconds of video
  • Written disclosure in video description before "Show More"
  • On-screen text during affiliate mentions (optional but recommended)
  • Use YouTube's "Includes paid promotion" feature

TikTok & Pinterest

  • TikTok: Use #ad or #affiliate visibly in caption
  • Pinterest: Include "affiliate link" in pin description
  • Both: Consider pinned comments with disclosures
  • Ensure disclosures are visible without clicking "Read More"

Advanced Considerations for Specific Situations

Multiple Affiliate Programs

If you participate in multiple affiliate programs, your disclosure should mention this:

"This website participates in multiple affiliate marketing programs. This means I may earn commissions on purchases made through my links to various retailer sites including but not limited to [List Major Programs]."

Content Upgrades and Lead Magnets

If you promote affiliate products within free downloads or email sequences:

  • Include disclosure within the downloadable content
  • Add disclosure to email footers when containing affiliate links
  • Consider separate disclosure for each product recommendation

International Audiences

If you have significant traffic from multiple countries:

  • Use language that meets the strictest requirements (typically FTC)
  • Consider geo-targeted disclosures if using different affiliate programs by country
  • Update for GDPR compliance if serving EU audiences

Maintenance and Updates

Affiliate disclosure isn't a one-time task. Maintain compliance with:

  1. Annual Review: Check for legal updates in your countries
  2. Program Updates: Update when adding/removing affiliate programs
  3. Date Stamps: Keep "Last Updated" date current on disclosure pages
  4. Testing: Regularly test disclosures on different devices
  5. Documentation: Keep records of your disclosure implementation

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I really need an affiliate disclosure on my website?

Yes, if you earn money through affiliate links. In the USA, the FTC requires clear disclosure. In the UK, ASA enforces similar rules. Canada, Australia, and EU countries have equivalent regulations. Failure to disclose can result in fines, legal action, and damaged credibility. It's both a legal requirement and ethical practice.

Where should I place my affiliate disclosure?

Place disclosures: 1) On every page with affiliate links (footer link to full page), 2) Immediately before affiliate links in content (best practice), 3) In your website navigation/footer for global access, 4) On dedicated disclosure page (required by Amazon Associates), 5) Near calls-to-action containing affiliate links. The key is proximity and clarity.

What wording should I use for my disclosure?

Use clear, simple language. Examples: 'This post contains affiliate links. I earn a commission if you make a purchase through my links, at no extra cost to you.' Or 'Disclosure: As an Amazon Associate I earn from qualifying purchases.' Avoid vague terms like 'partner links' or 'sponsored content' unless you explain what they mean.

Do social media posts need affiliate disclosures?

Absolutely. The FTC requires disclosures on all platforms where you share affiliate links: Instagram (#affiliate or #ad in first 3 lines), YouTube (verbal disclosure early in video + description), TikTok, Facebook, Twitter, and Pinterest. Platform-specific hashtags like #affiliatelink or #commissionearned are generally accepted if clearly visible.

What are the penalties for not disclosing affiliate relationships?

Penalties vary by country: USA (FTC fines up to $43,792 per violation), UK (ASA can require ad removal and public rulings), Canada (Competition Bureau fines), Australia (ACCC fines up to $1.1M for companies). Additionally, affiliate programs may terminate your account, and search engines may penalize your site.

How specific does my disclosure need to be?

Specific enough that a reasonable person understands: 1) That you earn money from links, 2) That it doesn't cost them extra, 3) That your opinions remain your own, 4) Which links are affiliate vs regular links if mixed. For Amazon Associates, you must use their required language exactly as specified.

Do I need separate disclosures for different countries?

If targeting multiple countries, your disclosure should meet the strictest requirements among them. A well-crafted disclosure that meets FTC standards (most stringent) typically satisfies other countries' requirements too. However, some programs like Amazon Associates require country-specific language for each marketplace.
